CARP Task

This was a fun task; however, it didn’t start off that way. I undeniably spent the most amount of time on my first design. For the longest time, I had included the warped “True/False Festival” heading as seen in my third poster. This didn’t look right. I was frustrated with how bad the poster looked like for a while. But after considering the emphasis of Contrast, Alignment, Repetition and Proximity, I decided to take a different approach. Two out of three posters included the contrast of the black text – white background/white text – red background. This can also be viewed as repetition, as it imitates the same colour scheme as the True/False logo. I also attempted to do this with the inclusion of the ‘scribbled’ black lines that enclose the text, which I feel also compliments and takes inspiration from the logo. However, with that being said, it also runs the risk of appearing too messy, and adding unnecessary busyness to the aesthetic. The repetition on the second poster can be seen with a 'true’ and ‘false’ print being repeated in the background behind the white text. Regarding alignment and proximity, I feel all of the text on the posters has visual connections between the backgrounds and headings accordingly. In my opinion, posters 2 and 3 include better proximity, with the location and date of the festival being just underneath the main heading. This allows the reader to quicky identify all of the key information from just an initial glance. Despite the third poster possibly coming across as being the simplest, as well as the most uninspired, I feel it is the most successful design overall. The design first takes the True/False logo, and extends the red and white division so it spans across the whole page. I’m proud of the contrast of the black text on the white and white text on red. Due to my limited experience with InDesign, it was difficult to create the 'colour split' effect within the same word, such as in “festival” and “Truefalse.org”. I had to opt to zooming in as close as possible and roughly covering the small portions of the letters that overlapped with rectangle shapes and lines from the pencil tool. I’m sure there must have been a quicker, easier way to achieve what I was going for, but I was unable to work it out.

My final poster stems from the third design in having a similar centered "T/F" logo. this time I decided to use an image, opposed to the more traditional logo from the previous three. By doing this I feel it adds depth to the poster. I also wanted to ensure I takled the issue of too much unused space, a problem i feel that the other posters suffer from. In my opinion, having a black background colour creates far better contrast. The heading, location and date fit together at the top in a jigsaw-like fashion, showing better arrangement and proximity, whilst the bottom text is arraged in a grid. I made the black boxes housing the text more transparent intentionally, just because I pefer the look of having the slight corners of the image below exposed.

Type Task

Axial

An axial design can be created by placing your text either side of an imaginary line (axis). This can either be an implicit or explicit axis, symmetrical or asymmetrical too.

Radial

Created by choosing an initial focal point and styling your text to “radiate” out of it. Can sometimes prove difficult to read, so establishing a clear hierarchy of text is important.

Dilatational

These are designs that are set to follow around a circular path. Can also be hard to read. If the text creates a full circle, then some of it will appear upside down.

Grid

Universal type system; works in just about any format. The text and the graphics can be arranged into neat columns and rows.

Transitional

Works better when used on posters or as a book cover. Resembles layers of sediment in the earth. Aiming for natural looking composition.

Bilateral

Bilateral designs are also known as text that is centred. It is often symmetrical as well. Can be made to look more interesting by utilising horizontal, vertical and non-objective elements.

Modular

Text that is layed out in repeating structures, such as within shapes. Perfect to use with pieces of content that have been already cut up.

Random

Essentially combining all of the above typographic systems and mashing them all into a layout that is either readable or completely illegible. Go nuts!

Out of all the tasks that were required for the assignment, the typography task was personally the easiest one to get on with. This was due to the online resources I was able to refer to and the uncomplicated, effortless layout of tools available on Illustrator. I initiated the task by taking on the Dilatational style first. Once I was able to locate the “type on path” tool, I could draw multiple circles and then creatively adjust the text to my liking. This tool was also useful when working in the Transitional style too, with the only difference being I would type on the path of lines that were created free-hand. Personally, I am satisfied with how the majority of the posters came out, particularly, again the Dilatational and Transitional styles. I also like the Random design as well because it was the most fun to create. The designs I’m not as pleased with are the Bilateral and Modular styles. The Bilateral one I feel is a bit too boring. I felt more restricted with this, as it’s simply centred text. I did experiment with moving a tilting the text, but it didn’t look as aesthetically pleasing, so I decided to make it a bit more interesting with the inclusion of the layered square shapes. I feel like I was also lacking creativity with the Modular design as well. Although it is up to par with the specifications, I believe it falls short in the sense of it not being as striking or imaginative as some of the other designs.

Reflection Report

For me this assignment felt like a jump in the deep end for the first time. I’ve had to quickly learn and adapt to new programs, software and then develop skills within these programs and honestly, I wasn’t sure whether I would be left floating, or if I'd sink. For a long time, I was cruising at a steady pace, not really taking into the consideration of time constraints. I was getting by, tackling the subjectively easier tasks first, so the initial one I started was the typography posters. However, very quickly I had found myself at the first obstacle: I had started creating the posters in InDesign, rather than Illustrator, as stated on the brief. But I can say I am satisfied with how these turned out in the end. Are they executed with intricacy and extreme attention to detail? Most definitely not, but that’s okay. In my opinion, I opted to take a more simplistic approach to this challenge and I feel there’s a clear correlation between each poster in terms of theme, which was exactly what I was going for. The coding worksheets came next. I was able to comfortably get on with the initial two, but when I came to seeing styling with CSS for the first time and linking other pages, I hesitated, and decided I would revisit it later. After this, I took on the CARP task. Again, rather slowly. I created a mock-up of the first poster and really didnt like the design I produced. I remember scanning the room, catching glimpses of the posters made my fellow classmates, thinking “mine is so bad compared to theirs”. It was at this point I came to the conclusion I would return to the coding task sheets and design a few wireframes and a flowchart for UX. And I'm glad I went back to this, because these coding sheets were imperative in the creation of my own website. I took inspiration from the Flexbox sheet and was able to make it my own somewhat successfully, I hope? Again, I cannot stress enough how perfect the worksheets were laid out. they were so easy to follow and contained a good number of challenges that required looking back and finding out. Going back to the posters, I am happy in the way I can see visual improvement, with each one being better than the last. Foolishly, I did leave the creation of the website dangerously late because of feeling of being lost and not knowing where or how to start. There was also added pressure from my peers seeing how amazing all of theirs were coming along after asking for advice. After allowing a moment to re-evaluate, I focused all my energy on getting everything done and polished before piecing it all together a week before the deadline in class time. For a simple design, I am proud of what I was able to eventually produce. I especially like how the tasks are divided into the exact main colours that were present in each individual task. This was a last-minute addition, but definitely one that adds the most aesthetic appeal.
